Chronically Unwilling to Guild Master

In the fourth episode of What's Your College Story?, I sat down with Karen Hyder — a trainer, event producer, speaker coach, virtual learning pioneer, and eLearning Guild Master whose résumé somehow still doesn't capture everything she's actually done. I've known Karen from my own L&D conference days, and I'll be honest: I knew her story was good. I didn't know it was this good.

Karen's path winds from a Catholic school in Buffalo to a college writing center, from teaching Turkish police officers English at a dining room table to pioneering online trainer certification in London, from cobbling together gig work in a farmhouse with satellite internet to helping shape the entire field of virtual eLearning. And it starts — as the best stories often do — with someone who really didn't want to be in school.

The Chronically Unwilling Student

Karen grew up near Buffalo, New York, attending a Catholic grammar school for nine years before moving on to an all-girls private high school run by nuns. She's quick to point out that private school didn't mean money flowed freely — once tuition was paid, she was largely on her own.

She wasn't a strong student. By her own description, she was far more interested in organizing things, planning events, and keeping her nose in a book. She once skipped school to finish a Stephen King novel. The Sisters of Mercy, she jokes, were very good at educating the chronically unwilling — and they got her ready for college, even if she wasn't sure she was.

Seven Jobs and a Literature Degree

At 17, Karen headed to college 100 miles from home — safe enough, and a school two of her siblings had attended. She started in psychology, switched to literature and writing when she realized the stories were more compelling than the program, and submitted papers through WordPerfect 3.0 for DOS. (Orange text on a black screen. She has not forgotten.)

While carrying a full course load across eight semesters, she worked seven different jobs: washing dishes, serving food in the dining hall, babysitting, waiting tables, working retail, doing handy work, and teaching English as a second language. She wasn't a student who also worked. She was a worker who also happened to be in school.

The job that changed everything was at the campus Writing Center, where she worked one-on-one with students trying to pass the Test for Competency in Writing — a graduation requirement that many had already failed at least once. She learned to meet people where they were: to offer specific guidance when needed and broader encouragement when confidence was the real barrier. That instinct has never left her.

From the Writing Center to the Dining Room Table

A small card on a bulletin board changed Karen's trajectory. Berlitz Language Schools was hiring English teachers. She took the job and found herself sitting across from businessmen from Kodak, Xerox, and Bausch & Lomb — from Japan, Brazil, Turkey, Germany, and Mexico — and then visiting their families at home to teach them too. Six Turkish police officers for nine months. Her mind, she says, was blown wide open.

"Kareeen, what means 'paper or plastic'? Why they ask me this when buy the food?"

One of her students told her: "Karen, when you first came to me, I didn't understand a word you said and I wanted to cry." By the time they had that conversation, there was no obstacle left between them. That moment — watching someone cross from confusion to fluency — is what teaching is for.

The Teddi Project and the Skills No Transcript Captures

Despite already working multiple jobs and carrying a full course load, Karen was recruited by an extroverted friend who decided she had too much free time. The result was the Teddi Project Dance for Love — a 24-hour fundraising marathon for Camp Good Days and Special Times, honoring Teddi Mervis, a local girl who had lost her battle with cancer.

Karen's job: coordinate enough musical acts to fill 24 hours, some performing in the middle of the night. She pulled together big bands, radio DJs, Arthur Murray Dance Studio, and one year, Lou Gramm from Foreigner — who couldn't sing due to his contract, but showed up anyway. Karen sang to him instead.

Karen's communications professor, Dr. Lou Buttino, later created a documentary production course with the Teddi story as the capstone project. They wrote, directed, filmed, and edited a documentary about Teddi's legacy. Demanding work — done for love and for credit.

None of this showed up on her transcript. But the skills she built — event coordination, volunteer management, speaker recruitment, production logistics — would define her career for the next thirty years.

"Her."

A few years after graduation, Karen walked into an interview at Logical Operations, a technology training company. They asked what she knew about computers. She knew WordPerfect for DOS. They asked her to teach them something.

She taught them the Berlitz Method: "This is a thing. What is this? It's a thing."

When she left the room, the panel of nine managers and trainers looked at each other and said, unanimously: "Her."

She stayed for nine years, eventually serving as Director of Trainer Development. She taught hundreds of learners each week from companies like Kodak, Xerox, and the University of Rochester — and trained trainers to do the same. The instructional model she worked from — Set Up, Delivery, Follow-Up — was a more learner-centered evolution of the old "tell them, tell them, tell them" approach. She later expanded it into her own framework, though she says that's a story for another time.

London, Monaco, and the Renegade Trainer

As her career grew, so did her travel schedule. Relentless road warrior years led Karen and her husband to make a leap: they moved to London, where she built a trainer certification program from scratch. She immersed herself in British culture, re-read her entire British literature syllabus, and immediately started telling trainers something that felt almost revolutionary at the time: come out from behind the podium. Stop lecturing. Ask questions and wait for answers.

She also studied Neuro-Linguistic Programming with Richard Bandler, Paul McKenna, and Michael Breen — an experience she calls mind-blowing — and reconnected with conference producers Heidi Fisk and David Holcombe. Through their Influent conferences in Barcelona and Monaco, she presented sessions, coached speakers, and led tracks, recruiting presenters to speak for free. The same skills. Different cities. Multimillion-dollar yachts visible from the reception rooftop.

The Farmhouse, the Satellite Internet, and the Future of Training

Eventually, Karen and her husband needed a reset. They bought an old house on a hilltop and began restoring it — splitting wood, digging in the dirt, and living on satellite internet that was good for email and not much else.

She kept working, cobbling together gig projects, until a client from Morgan Stanley called with an unusual request: their trainers were exhausted from flying to China. Could she build a train-the-trainer program using PlaceWare — a precursor to Microsoft Live Meeting? She could. She did. It was 1999, and it was the beginning of her life as a virtual learning professional.

For a while, she couldn't even teach from home — she drove ten miles to a rented office to access a wired connection, sometimes working in the middle of the night to align with time zones overseas. But she had found what she'd been imagining for years: a way to teach from anywhere.

Shaping a Field

In 2004, Karen began working with the eLearning Guild — leading webinars because she was one of the only people actively teaching others how to teach online. What started as one day with three sessions grew into a major annual program. She became an online event producer and speaker coach, helping shape the emerging field of virtual eLearning at the moment it was being invented.

She contributed to books, wrote introductions and reviews — her blurbs appear on seven covers — and co-authored a chapter on accessibility in virtual classrooms for Design for All Learners. She joined the board overseeing trainer certification and helped push for a virtual credential: the Virtual Certified Trainer (VCT+). In 2017, she was honored as an eLearning Guild Master.

For the past decade, she has worked with Hearing First, a nonprofit producing live online learning experiences for professionals who work with deaf and hard-of-hearing children and their families.

The Lightning Round

At the end of every episode, I ask guests two questions and one more for fun.

Karen's favorite thing to learn? Other cultures and language. Give her someone willing to teach her about where they come from, and she will sit and listen to every word.

The hardest? Financial planning as a gig worker. When income swings wildly from month to month, you get very good at MacGyvering your finances — or you don't survive. Karen has developed her own system: plan backwards from the worst case, keep conservative reserves, and don't lose sleep the night before a live event. She has techniques for that too.

And the one thing she wants more people to know about her? She uses vision boards. Has for years. She knows it sounds woo-woo. She doesn't care. They help her stay focused on what she actually wants — and get better at saying no to what she doesn't.

(For the record: we both agreed that "woo woo" is, itself, an idiom. Karen awarded me a wooden nickel.)

Why Karen's Story Matters to Elikonas

Karen's career is a near-perfect illustration of what Elikonas is being built to recognize. Seven jobs through college. A literature degree she describes as "really a psychology degree." A writing center job that became a Berlitz teaching career that became a nine-year training directorship. A 24-hour dance marathon that became a lifelong skill in event production. Sabbaticals. Gig work. A farmhouse. London. Monaco. A certification she helped invent.

None of that is on her transcript.

Today, there's no single place where a story like Karen's can be told in full — where every thread connects, where the Writing Center and the Monaco rooftop and the 1999 PlaceWare course and the Guild Master honor all belong to the same person and the same journey. Elikonas is building that place. Because Karen's story doesn't fit in a box. It never did. And it never should have had to.
