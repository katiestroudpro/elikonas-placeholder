The Plant Lady Knows What She Knows

In the fifth episode of What's Your College Story?, Katie sat down with Christabel Behr — childhood friend, landscape designer, and the person Katie calls when her roses need saving. They've been friends since fifth grade, lost touch for a while when Katie left for college, and have found their way back to each other in recent years. Christabel lives about three hours south of Seattle, has a job she genuinely loves, and has spent the better part of her adult life proving that a degree isn't the only way to become an expert in something.

Her story starts with a dream — a very specific, very vivid dream of campus life — and a family that didn't quite know what to do with it. In her own words:

About Christabel Behr

[Christabel's short bio placeholder]

The Campus Dream

I really, really wanted to go to college and I wanted to have a professional degree. My mom was a stay-at-home mom who didn't start working until I was 15 — she was kind of confused about the process too. We were learning those skills at the same time.

When I was 10 years old, my family attended religious conventions at OSU in Corvallis. We'd stay in the dorms for a few days each summer, and I just fell in love with it — the campus, the shared bathrooms that probably nobody else was excited about, sitting out on the lawn, the little art shop down below where I wanted to go every year. I just really wanted that immersive studying life, where you get really good at something. I'd watch the Brady Bunch and see Mr. Brady come home to a family that was calm and happy, and think: I want that kind of life.

I told my dad when I was 10 that I wanted to go to college. He said he'd pay for it. What I didn't find out until high school was that my parents hadn't saved a single dollar toward that promise — and they really didn't see the need for it.

The Car vs. College

I didn't understand how much college cost, and I didn't have a car, no money to start out, no money for a place to live. My entire family, when I brought it up, had one answer: you need a car. Not college. A car. Every single person said the same thing.

So I ended up getting the car. And college came slower, in bits and pieces, over the years.

The one break I caught was Running Start — a Washington State program where you earn both high school and college credit at the same time. Clark College happened to be right across the street from my high school, so I didn't need a car to get there. I was in high school talking to a counselor, telling her I really wanted to go to college but didn't know how any of it worked — the money, the grants, the applications. A classmate happened to be walking by and the counselor pulled him aside and said, "Can you take her?" That's how it started. Not because I had a plan. Because someone pointed me in a direction and I walked.

The Learning That Never Stopped

What followed was years — decades, really — of gathering knowledge from every direction Christabel could find. Here's the list, because it deserves to be seen in full:

A floristry certificate from Clark College. An AAS in Landscape Technology from Clark College — two years, with her parents covering half the tuition and Christabel working 30 hours a week to cover her half, debt-free by design. A Master Gardener certification through WSU. A Master Pruner certification through Plant Amnesty, a year-long program affiliated with a Seattle college — one she can't say enough good things about — which brought her up to the Seattle area regularly, including a visit to Katie's yard where she pruned roses and blueberry bushes that grew beautifully afterward. A pre-apprenticeship program through Oregon Tradeswomen, where she tried welding, electrical work, woodworking, and got a feel for construction site life. The Regenerative Leadership Institute's permaculture design certification. Wild Food Adventures with John Kayles (PhD, nutrition), learning to identify and use edible plants in the field. Botany in a Day — a book and online program with participants from around the world. A landscape design program at PCC, just across the Oregon border. And an attempt at VectorWorks — the AutoCAD program for landscape design — that she'd like to revisit someday with a better textbook and a more patient instructor.

None of this adds up to a four-year degree. All of it adds up to a career.

What She Actually Wanted

The thing Christabel always wanted — the thing that pulled her toward plants in the first place — was botany. Plant identification. The universal language that connects people across every barrier.

At 19, she traveled to Egypt and walked through a botanical garden where she couldn't speak the language of the other visitors. But they both knew botanical Latin. They walked the whole garden together, talking about plants. What a wonderful thing, she thought, to have a universal language about something so wonderful in the world.

She never got the botany degree. She did floristry first because she didn't quite realize it wasn't what she was looking for. Then landscape technology. Then more and more certifications, always chasing the thing she'd imagined at 10 years old — sitting on that campus lawn, wanting to get really good at something.

This year, she told Katie, is finally the year she feels like she can stop gathering. She has the basis. Now she can start doing the work and making the most of everything she's learned — and she has zero college debt to carry while doing it.

The Boss Who Handed Her a Catalog

About eleven years ago, Christabel started working for a landscape company. On her first day, the owner — an entrepreneur with no college degree himself — handed her a thick plant catalog and said: "I believe in self-education. Here. Figure it out."

She was intimidated. Then she started looking things up. How big does this plant get? When does it flower? Does the flower have a scent? She spent the first two years learning on her own, from a book and from the soil. Now people call Christabel — the Plant Lady — when they need something identified. When they need to know what will grow where. When they want to get it right.

Today she manages roughly $150,000 in plant purchases a year, places plants on job sites, meets with homeowners to understand their needs, and hand-drafts landscape designs. The math is constant — square footage, delivery loads, native plant percentages for habitat certifications, spacing, materials. Her first independent paid design project is underway: helping a client achieve backyard natural habitat certification, working toward 5 to 50 percent native plant coverage on a large property.

She loves it. That was always the goal — to spend her days doing something she loves. Not everybody gets that. But it's what she worked toward.

The Lightning Round

Hardest thing to learn? VectorWorks — and everything it represents. Sitting at a desk. Not being outside. Not having her hands in the dirt. That's the part that doesn't come naturally.

Favorite thing to learn? Botany. Plant ID. Being outside, touching things, smelling things. Knowing the name of what's growing next to you and what it needs and what it offers. As she put it: "It really puts you in touch with the planet."

Would she do it differently if money and transportation had never been obstacles? Her answer was honest and a little complicated: No — but also, there could have been a whole different life she couldn't even have imagined. Two thoughts, clashing. Most people who've taken the long way give some version of the same answer.

One thing college can't teach, even if it tries? How to interact with people in public. "That's something you learn by doing it." No classroom can give you that.

Why Christabel's Story Matters to Elikonas

Christabel has a file somewhere where she keeps all her certifications. She mentioned it during the interview — she'd have to go look for it. They're scattered across programs, institutions, parks, gardens, and years. There's no single place where the floristry certificate and the permaculture design certification and the wild food adventures and the Master Pruner training and the Egypt story all live together and tell the complete picture of who she is and what she knows.

That's the problem Elikonas is building to solve. Christabel described it perfectly herself: "You have all these things which maybe add up to a lot more years than a four-year degree, but you don't have a way to explain them, or you have ten or fifteen different certifications and you want to condense that into one thing — just one place that shows what you have overall."

That place doesn't exist yet. Elikonas is building it. Because Christabel Behr is the Plant Lady. She knows what she knows. And she's earned every bit of it — just not in any way a transcript would recognize.

— Katie
